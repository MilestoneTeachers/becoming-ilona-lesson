A Letter to a Seven-Year-Old

After we read about Judy at seven, write a short letter about names to a seven-year-old you know now.

Think of a seven-year-old in your life right now. A little sibling. A cousin. The child of a family friend. A neighbour. Not a stranger. Someone whose face you can picture. Write them a short handwritten letter about names. Where yours came from. Why names matter. What you want them to know about carrying their own name, especially when someone tries to change it or shorten it or use it as a joke. The audience is a real child today, not Judy. Judy's story is the reason we are writing. The seven-year-old in your life is the reader.

HowThree small instructions

  1. Address the letter to one specific child. Use their name.
  2. Tell them where your name came from, in one or two sentences.
  3. Then tell them one thing about why names are worth protecting. Keep it small. A whole life can fit into one careful sentence.
Dear ,

Love,

When you are done. Fold the letter. Put it somewhere. You do not have to send it. The letter is the practice, not the delivery. If you do want to give it to the child you addressed it to, ask their parent first. Some seven-year-olds will read it on the spot. Some will save it. Some will not be old enough to understand what you wrote about names today, and will understand it years from now. All of those are fine.

Why we wrote it. Judy was seven when she was told that her name would be Ilona for the year ahead, because being named Judit Grünfeld in 1944 Budapest could get her killed. Most of you have never had to consider that your name itself could put you in danger. The letter you just wrote is what it sounds like when a community decides, generation after generation, that names are worth carrying carefully forward. That is the work this lesson is asking of you, in a form you can hand to a seven-year-old.

Source guidance: United States Holocaust Memorial Museum, Guidelines for Teaching About the Holocaust, Guideline 10 (avoid simulation); International Holocaust Remembrance Alliance, Recommendations for Teaching and Learning about the Holocaust, 2019, § 3.2.7. The letter-to-a-child prompt is a Yad Vashem-recommended structure for elementary Holocaust pedagogy: the writer remains a witness, not a victim, and the act of writing crosses generations.
