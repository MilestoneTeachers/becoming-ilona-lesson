Becoming Ilona · Student Exhibit 2 of 3 Auditory · 10 minutes

Sound Map of 1944 Budapest

What did the city sound like, in three places a seven-year-old might have stood, in the year Judy became Ilona?

Historians and memoirists describe places by what they looked like. We are going to describe three places from Judy's wartime childhood by what they sounded like. Read the short context for each zone. Use the context to fill in three to five sounds you think a child would have heard. Some sounds will be obvious. Some will be hidden in the context. Some you will have to guess at, carefully. This is research, not simulation. You are not Judy. You are a careful listener mapping the sound world of a real place at a real time.

1Inside a Jewish home in Budapest, early 1944

Before the German occupation in March 1944, Jewish families in Budapest still lived in their own apartments. A typical Jewish home in the Pest district was on an upper floor of a six-storey building, with a tile floor, a coal stove, a radio in the living room, and a piano against one wall in the families that had one. The city tram ran past the building below. Bread was delivered from a bakery on the same street. The radio carried state news bulletins in Hungarian and, after March, German military announcements.

2Inside the Ursuline Mother House, Stefánia Street, summer 1944

The Ursuline Mother House where Judy was hidden was a working Catholic convent: long stone corridors, classrooms, a chapel, a courtyard, dormitories for boarding pupils. The convent ran on a bell schedule, with prayer at fixed hours. Children walked in lines from dormitory to refectory to chapel. The Latin Mass was sung at sunrise. The nuns wore long habits and rosaries that clicked against keys. Beyond the convent walls, on Stefánia Street, trolleybuses ran past and military trucks moved through the city.

3A street corner in Budapest, autumn 1944, after the Arrow Cross takeover

On October 15, 1944, the Hungarian fascist Arrow Cross party took control of Budapest. The city changed sound overnight. Curfew sirens. Loudspeakers mounted on lamp posts broadcasting new orders. Boots on cobblestones. Soviet artillery moving closer from the east week by week. The trams still ran. Carl Lutz, the Swiss diplomat who saved tens of thousands of Hungarian Jews, was still issuing protection passes from a building on Vádasz Street. Snow was beginning.

NoticeOne sound that surprised you

Look back at the three zones. Pick one sound on your list that surprised you when you thought of it. Write a sentence about why it stays with you. You will not be asked to share unless you want to.

What this exhibit is doing. Judy was a real child in real places. Real places have real sounds. When we list the sounds she would have heard, we are not pretending to be her. We are honouring the specificity of where she actually was. That is what historians and memoirists do. That is what you are doing here.
