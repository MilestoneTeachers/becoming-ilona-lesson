Two-Names Identity Map

Mapping the two selves you already carry, before we read about Judy's two names.

Most people carry more than one name in a day. The name your family uses at the dinner table. The name a teacher reads off the attendance list. A nickname only one friend is allowed to call you. A heritage name from a grandparent. A weekday self and a weekend self. Two languages. Two ways of being at home. This exhibit is not about Judy. It is about you. Fill it in as yourself. When you are done, we will look at how Judy carried Judit and Ilona for almost a year, and the work the two-name map will already have done in your head.

Name You Use Most

Another Name You Carry

Judy's parallel. In 1944, in a convent in Budapest, a seven-year-old named Judit Grünfeld was given a second name, Ilona Papp, to keep her alive. Judit was her family name, her Jewish name, the name her mother used. Ilona was the name she had to answer to in the convent, in Catholic prayers, in the courtyard, in front of strangers. She carried both for almost a year. When we read page 10 of Tenuous Threads together, hold the map you just filled in. The map is what makes Judy's sentence land. You already know what it is to carry two names. Judy carried hers in a place where one of them could not be said out loud.

You do not have to share any of this with the class. The exhibit is yours. If you want to keep the page, fold it. If you want to throw it away, throw it away. The point was the thinking, not the paper.

Source guidance for trauma-informed identity-mapping in Holocaust education: United States Holocaust Memorial Museum, Guidelines for Teaching About the Holocaust; International Holocaust Remembrance Alliance, Recommendations for Teaching and Learning about the Holocaust, 2019, § 3.2.7 (avoid simulation, use parallel inquiry from the student's own life).
