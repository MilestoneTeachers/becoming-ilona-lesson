Student Exhibits

Three short interactive companions to the lesson, one for each way a Grade 6 student learns best.

Each exhibit is a single page, prints cleanly, and runs ten to fifteen minutes. Teachers can assign all three across a unit, or pick the one that matches the way a particular class enters new material. All three sit inside the lesson's trauma-informed frame: the student is a witness, learner, or memory-keeper, never asked to perform Judy's life. The exhibits extend the lesson into students' own bodies, ears, and hands without putting them inside a hidden child's experience.

  • Visual10 minutesSits with Minds On

    Two-Names Identity Map

    Students map two of their own identities, side by side, on the page: the name they use most, and another name they carry (a nickname, a heritage name, a name only one person uses). The map fills in for each name: who calls them that, what places it belongs in, what feeling it carries. Judy's parallel of Judit and Ilona appears at the end as the lesson's mirror, not the exhibit's subject.

  • Auditory10 minutesSits with Reading I

    Sound Map of 1944 Budapest

    Three real zones from Judy's wartime city, each with a historian's-paragraph of context: a Jewish home in early 1944, the Ursuline convent on Stefánia Street, a Budapest street corner after the Arrow Cross takeover. Students fill in the sounds a child would have heard, grounded in the context, listening their way into a real place. Research, not simulation.

  • Kinesthetic, hands-on15 minutesSits with Reflection

    A Letter to a Seven-Year-Old

    After we read about Judy at seven, students handwrite a short letter about names to a seven-year-old they actually know now: a sibling, a cousin, the child of a family friend. The audience is a real living child today; the student is the writer; Judy's story is the reason. The letter does not have to be sent. The writing is the work.
